Research

Undergraduate Research Intern, FICUS lab

PI: Professor Sarah Kavassalis

Sept 2023 – Aug 2025

    The FICUS Lab's research focuses on flexible, interdisciplinary computational approaches for understanding the atmosphere. I studied the interpretability of long short-term memory models (LSTM) used to predict ozone levels, and evaluated the effectiveness of the explainable AI (XAI) technique layerwise relevance propagation (LRP).

    To do this, I processed and restructured air quality datasets. I also tuned LSTM hyperparameters to find the optimal hyperparameters for ozone prediction. Then, I analyzed LRP results across multiple LSTM initializations, diurnal and seasonal cycles, and compared against random forest baselines (using libraries like Pandas & SHAP).

Undergraduate Research Intern, Gerbode Lab for Soft Matter Physics

PI: Professor Sharon Gerbode

Jan 2022 – March 2024

    The Gerbode Lab for Soft Matter Physics focuses on understanding the fundamental mechanisms that influence aging in small crystals. I specifically studied grain boundaries, which are a property of small crystals responsible for determining many bulk material properties, which can have applications in materials like photovoltaics. Despite the well-known importance of grain boundaries, the established theories that describe how grain boundaries move, were developed for large crystals in the continuum limit. As technology pushes us toward smaller and smaller systems, we need a better understanding of the atomic scale mechanisms that enable grain boundary motion in smaller crystals.

    During my research in this lab, I developed several scripts analyzing the properties of granule dissolution like cooperative motion, vorticity, and misorientation angle. I successfully found that the size of rotating granules during vorticity was inversely related to misorientation angle, like the underlying Moiré pattern in the crystal lattice. I’ve also successfully identified cooperative motion, a mechanism where particles follow each other as they move, as a signature of dissolution. My work also involved creating ramp cells (plates of colloidal particles) and employing optical blasting to create grain boundaries for physical observation.

    Furthermore, I co-authored a peer-reviewed American Physical Society (APS) publication, and presenting a talk at the APS March Meeting in 2024.

Team Lead & Clinic Intern, LS Power

Aug 2024 – May 2025

    I led a team of six to optimize the gas-capture process of methane at a landfill-to-renewable gas purification plant through analysis of landfill sensor data. The data contained 10+ parameters for 100+ sensors over the course of several years. This data was quite noisy, and had many gaps, so we employed techniques like filtering and interpolation during processing. I realized that to properly identify broken sensors, we needed to perform trend analysis that combined methods from statistical process control, feature engineering, and machine learning. We also needed to embed domain knowledge from site technicians, like constraints on parameter values, directly into our model. My work in developing this combined-approach pipeline resulted in the team being able to predict broken sensors up to a week in advance. In addition, the team prototyped a user interface to facilitate real-time tracking of well status, as this was a dataset that would have been useful to the overall analysis. Serving as team lead taught me to communicate results to engineers, site technicians, and industry experts, reinforcing the importance of interpretable tools.
